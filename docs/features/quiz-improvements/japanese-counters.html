② Why 分 (minutes) misbehaves

The minute-counter isn't random — it follows a euphony rule based on how the number's last sound meets ふん. Grouping by trigger sound turns six "random" exceptions into two rules.

1, 6, 8, 10 end in a "stop" sound
→
ふん hardens to っぷん (geminate + p) ippun · roppun · happun · juppun
2, 5, 7, 9 end in a vowel-ish sound
→
ふん stays plain nifun · gofun · nanafun · kyuufun
3 ん-adjacent
→
ふん shifts to ぷん (no gemination, just p) sanpun
4 already ends in ん
→
no change needed yonpun

Same っぷ-hardening shows up in 歳: issai, hassai, jussai are the same three "stop-sound" numbers (1, 8, 10) doing the same thing — plus 6 in 時 territory doesn't apply there, but the 1/6/8/10 cluster is worth remembering as a set, not four separate exceptions.
