It's not memorization.
It's multiplication.

English numbers are full of irregular words — "eleven," "twelve," "twenty" don't spell out their arithmetic. Japanese numbers do. Every number from 1 to 10,000 is built from just nine digits and four place values, combined the way you'd read an equation.

十 · 百 · 千 alone already mean "10 / 100 / 1,000" — you never say ichijuu, ichihyaku, or ichisen. But 万 alone is ambiguous, so 10,000 is always ichiman (一万), never bare 万.

05 — THE RESET

万 changes the rules of grouping

English commas split numbers into groups of three zeros (thousand, million, billion). Japanese splits into groups of four — because 万 (10,000), not 千 (1,000), is the next real unit. This is why 10,000 feels like a bigger conceptual jump in Japanese than "ten thousand" does in English.

English grouping (÷1,000)

9,999 → 10,000
A new comma group starts. "Ten thousand" is still built from "thousand" — no new word.

Japanese grouping (÷10,000)

九千九百九十九 → 一万
A genuinely new unit, 万, takes over. Everything up to 9999 was built from 千 (sen); now counting restarts on man.
06 — SCALING 万

万 takes a coefficient too

Once you're past 10,000, 万 stops being a fixed word and becomes a place value like any other — you put a digit (or a whole compound number) in front of it, exactly as you did with 十 · 百 · 千 back in section 02.

The trick: whatever number sits in front of 万 is itself built with the ordinary 千 · 百 · 十 · 一 rules from sections 02–03. 23万 is just "23" (二十三, nijuusan) with 万 stitched onto its last digit → 二十三万 (nijuusanman) = 230,000. Sound changes still apply too: 300万 → 三百万 (sanbyakuman), not "sanhyakuman." This pattern repeats one more time at 100,000,000, where 万 hands off to a fifth unit, 億 (oku) — outside this page's scope, but built from the exact same logic.
一 · 十 · 百 · 千 · 万 — the same five characters carry you from 1 to 9,999万 and beyond.
